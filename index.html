About Me

Hello! I'm Simran Makariye, an MSCS student at NYU Courant. I have completed my bachelor's degree in computer science at NIT Trichy. From 2020 to 2022, I worked as a software engineer at Qualcomm. I was a member of the location technology team and focused on various positioning algorithms related to delivering highly accurate location information. But my deep-rooted interest in the field of machine learning has always motivated me to seek further exploration, leading me to pursue a master's degree at NYU Courant.

My research interests lie in the captivating realms of deep learning and computer vision. I have been actively exploring the applications of vision in diverse fields, such as computational neuroscience, finance, and remote sensing.

Presently, I am fortunate to be working with Professor Eva Dyer from the Georgia Institute of Technology. Our research focuses on utilizing deep learning techniques to study cortical inter-areal and intra-areal interactions. This work has allowed me to contribute to the understanding of the complexities of the brain and how deep learning can shed light on these intricate mechanisms.

Additionally, I am gaining valuable experience as a summer research assistant under the guidance of Professor Vasant Dhar at NYU Stern. In this role, I am immersing myself in the world of stock price activity analysis, leveraging the power of deep learning to uncover patterns and insights that contribute to a deeper understanding of financial markets.

Furthermore, I am also involved in a part-time research project with Professor Grace Lindsay at NYU's Data Science Institute (CDS). Our collaboration specifically centers around the application of computer vision models in the domain of binary/multi-class object segmentation, utilizing multiple revsiits of low-resolution satellite images. This multidisciplinary approach highlights my versatility and ability to apply vision-based models across different domains.

I am always open to collaborate with like-minded individuals who share my passion for deep learning and computer vision. If you have any intriguing research projects or work opportunities that align with my interests, please do reach out. I am eager to continue learning and making meaningful contributions to these exciting fields.

My CV

Projects

Integrated Content Management Platform - New York Public Library

Developing a generalized content management pipeline for the NYPL website, incorporating deep learning techniques to enhance collection item accessibility and enable automated meta-data tagging. Also, integrating crowdsourcing technologies to further refine and optimize these solutions, resulting in improved user experience and streamlined content management processes.

Multi-Image Fusion and Semantic Segmentation for Power Substation Delineation

Collaborating with Transition Zero, a leading clean energy technology company, to develop deep neural models that fuse multiple revisits of low-resolution Sentinel-2 L1C imagery and generate a super-resolved image and then applying semantic segmentation task to accurately delineate power substations from the super-resolved image.

Video Frame Prediction and Semantic Segmentation with Self-Supervised Learning

Implemented a fully CNN architecture - SimVP - for video frame prediction and trained a U-Net architecture for Semantic Segmentation on a diverse synthetic dataset consisting of video clips of 3D moving objects achieving a Jaccard Index of 0.251 with minimal training and fine-tuning.

Semantic Role Labeling on NomBank Dataset

Implemented a fully CNN architecture - SimVP - for video frame prediction and trained a U-Net architecture for Semantic Segmentation on a diverse synthetic dataset consisting of video clips of 3D moving objects achieving a Jaccard Index of 0.251 with minimal training and fine-tuning.

Smart Mental Health Monitoring using Wearable Sensors

Designed and implemented an LSTM-based stress detection model using time-series data consisting of both wrist features and chest features emulated as wrist features. Achieved a remarkable accuracy of 90%.

Human Neuron Inspired CNN architecture for Music Emotion Recognition

Designed a CNN-based Music Emotion Recognition model that mimics the way a human brain responds to an emotion i.e. by firing a specific set of neurons for each emotion. It yielded an accuracy of 82% with the additional benefit of fewer computational parameters.
